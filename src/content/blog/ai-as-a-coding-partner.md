For the past year, AI has been sitting next to me in my IDE like the most patient senior developer I've ever worked with. It doesn't get tired, it doesn't judge my messy commits, and it has read more code than any human ever could. But here's the thing: it's not writing my software. I am.

## The Shift in How I Work

Before AI-assisted tools became mainstream, my workflow was straightforward: read documentation, write code, test, debug, repeat. The loop was slow, especially when I hit unfamiliar territory - a new Laravel feature, a tricky SQL optimization, or a frontend state management puzzle.

Now, the loop looks different. I still design the architecture. I still make the trade-offs. But when I'm stuck on a specific implementation detail, I ask. When I need to scaffold a CRUD system, I describe what I want and iterate. When I'm debugging a cryptic error at 2 AM, I paste the stack trace and get a second pair of eyes instantly.

The AI doesn't replace my thinking - it accelerates it.

## What AI Does Well

**Boilerplate and scaffolding.** Building a new Laravel resource with controllers, routes, validation rules, and Inertia views used to take an hour. Now it takes ten minutes of describing the domain model and refining the output.

**Debugging unfamiliar errors.** When SQL Server throws a cryptic collation error or Vue.js renders something I didn't expect, AI helps me narrow down the cause faster than scrolling through Stack Overflow threads.

**Explaining complex concepts.** Need to understand how `async/await` actually works under the hood in PHP? Or why a specific database index is slowing down your query? AI breaks it down in context, using your actual code as the example.

**Code review before humans see it.** I run my code through AI before opening a pull request. It catches obvious issues, suggests cleaner patterns, and sometimes spots security concerns I missed.

## What AI Does Poorly

**Architecture decisions.** AI will happily suggest a microservices architecture for a simple blog or a complex event-driven system when a straightforward CRUD app would do. It doesn't understand your business constraints, team size, or maintenance budget.

**Understanding the full picture.** It sees the file you paste. It doesn't see the legacy codebase, the angry client email, the deadline pressure, or the team dynamics that should influence your solution.

**Creativity and taste.** Building a brand experience like Killa or designing a survey flow in Quaestio requires human judgment about what feels right. AI can generate variations, but it can't feel the product.

**Responsibility.** When the production system breaks at 3 AM, the AI isn't the one getting paged. You are.

## My Daily Workflow

Here's how AI fits into an average day:

1. **Morning planning:** I outline what I need to build. The AI doesn't plan for me - I do.
2. **Implementation:** I write the core logic myself. For boilerplate, repetitive patterns, or unfamiliar APIs, I use AI to generate a starting point and then refine it.
3. **Debugging:** When something breaks, I investigate first. If I'm stuck after 15 minutes, I describe the symptoms to AI and work through the suggestions critically.
4. **Learning:** When I encounter a new concept, I ask AI to explain it with examples from my own codebase. This contextual learning is far more effective than generic tutorials.
5. **Review:** Before committing, I ask AI to review the diff. It catches things I miss when I'm deep in the flow.

## The Mindset Shift

The biggest change isn't technical - it's psychological. Using AI effectively requires learning to ask the right questions, to evaluate answers critically, and to know when to ignore the suggestion and trust your own judgment.

It's like pair programming with someone who knows every library ever written but has never shipped a product. They have infinite knowledge but zero context. Your job is to provide the context and make the calls.

## The Future

I don't believe AI will replace developers. I believe developers who use AI will replace developers who don't. The role is shifting from "person who types code" to "person who directs intelligent systems toward meaningful outcomes."

The best developers I know are already treating AI as a thinking partner, not a typing replacement. They're faster, they're more confident exploring unfamiliar territory, and they spend more time on the interesting problems instead of the repetitive ones.

That's the future I want to work in. Not one where humans are obsolete, but one where humans are amplified.
