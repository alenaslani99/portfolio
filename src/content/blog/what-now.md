I passed my last exam a few weeks ago. The desk is still cluttered with notes I'll never read again. The coffee shop where I studied now feels like someone else's regular spot. And the question that should have an answer - what now? - just sits there, louder than any alarm clock.

## The Gap Nobody Warns You About

School gives you a rhythm. Wake up, attend lectures, study, pass exams, repeat. There's a structure to it, a predictable arc. But nobody tells you about the silence that follows the final grade. The moment when the system stops holding your hand and you realize the next step isn't printed on any syllabus.

For years, my worth was measured in points. Now the scoreboard is gone and I'm left with a skillset, some projects, and a lot of uncertainty about where they fit in the world.

## The Pressure to Have It Figured Out

Social media makes it look like everyone else has a plan. Internships lined up, startups launched, clear paths mapped from graduation to corner office. But that's a highlight reel. The reality for most of us is messier. We know what we can do, but not where we belong yet. We have capabilities without context, potential without a stage.

The gap isn't empty. It's full of options, and that's what makes it heavy.

## What I've Learned So Far

**You don't need the perfect job.** You need the next job that teaches you something. The first role after school isn't a life sentence. It's a data point. You learn what you like, what you tolerate, and what you'll never do again. Each piece of information sharpens the picture.

**The projects that felt like practice were actually building your portfolio.** Every Laravel app, every Vue component, every SQL query optimized at 2 AM - they weren't just assignments. They were proof that you can build things that work. That matters more than any GPA.

**It's okay to not know.** The developers I respect most didn't have it figured out at 22 or 25 or 30. They tried things, failed at some, succeeded at others, and slowly assembled a career that made sense in retrospect. Clarity comes from motion, not from thinking harder.

## Building in the Dark

So what now? For me, it's about continuing to build while the path reveals itself. Ship projects. Write code that solves real problems. Contribute to things that matter. The gap between school and "real life" isn't a waiting room. It's just life, without the grading curve.

I'm starting to see that the question isn't "what now?" as much as "what next?" And the beautiful, terrifying thing is: I get to answer that myself.

The gradebook is closed. The canvas is blank. And somehow, that's exactly where I want to be.
